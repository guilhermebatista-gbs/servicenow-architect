# Delivery Quality Review — checkpoints for a healthy project

A delivery-quality (or "delivery excellence") review checks that the delivery methodology is actually being followed, so the team can correct course early. It can be run by an independent quality function or by the delivery manager. The idea is simple: at planned points, an examiner asks for evidence that the right things were produced and decisions were recorded.

## How to plan the reviews

- At project start, decide how many sessions are needed and schedule them (put them in the project plan).
- The delivery manager (or quality function) schedules, runs, and documents each session, and makes an action plan to protect quality.
- The EM, BPC, and TC prepare beforehand and bring the evidence.

Suggested cadence by project length:

| Project length | Review points |
|---|---|
| Short (≈3–5 months) | Initiate+Plan · Execute+Deliver+Close |
| Medium (≈6–10 months) | Initiate+Plan · Execute · Deliver+Close |
| Long (10+ months) | Initiate+Plan · Execute (n times) · Deliver+Close |

Eligibility: prioritize strategic, high-visibility, complex, or pioneering projects (large accounts, new products, complex environments).

## Evidence to request, by phase

**Initiate**
- Knowledge-transfer / intake document
- Scope document (SOW, kick-off deck, order form, or equivalent — the initial scope statement)
- Business objectives and goals (from a value assessment, or mapped in Initiate)
- Architecture design document (which platform modules will be implemented, and integrations)
- To-be process mapping (workshop output) with a written process description
- Kick-off deck; Initiate phase-gate

**Plan / Execute**
- Product/Epic/Theme/Story hierarchy (backlog configured correctly)
- Scope-checkpoint evidence (mandatory in Plan; in Execute only if scope changed)
- Plan phase-gate
- A risk/issue/decision/action-item log (RIDAC/RAID), maintained across all phases

**Execute**
- Health-check / sprint-review evidence
- Agile burndown (team performance measure)
- Execute phase-gate

**Deliver**
- Release descriptions (modules/features per release **and the business value delivered**)
- Instance/production readiness assessment (requested well ahead of go-live, sized to expected volume)
- A living register of strategic decisions and customizations
- Knowledge-transfer and training materials
- Deliver phase-gate

**Close**
- Close-out deck (including the strategic-decisions summary and a proposed backlog roadmap)
- Success-case record
- Close phase-gate

## When the customer refuses a best practice

Customers usually have the final say on how their solution is built; the delivery team's responsibility is to give options and document the risks and consequences. A workable playbook:

1. **Argue it with pros and cons** — show the trade-offs concretely.
2. **Explain why the best practice is future-proof** — especially the upgrade/maintenance cost of the alternative.
3. **Document the risks** if it isn't adopted.
4. **Ask for help** — bring in the account team, product specialists, or leadership when platform/security/compliance is at stake.
5. **Document the decision** — a formal "DECISION" note (email + solution deck attached + consequences), logged in the RIDAC and linked on the affected stories.
6. **Make sure all stakeholders know** the decision and its consequences.
7. **Recall every such decision at close-out** — so nothing is a surprise later.

The goal isn't to win every argument; it's to make sure decisions are informed, explicit, owned, and traceable.
